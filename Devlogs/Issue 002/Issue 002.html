How I made Keystroke

Hey fellas, it's been about 3 weeks since I've made Keystroke, and I wanted to go over my thought process during the game's development.


Originally, I wanted to make something a little more fun and engaging than my previous game, Gold Rush, was. While I had the goal of making Gold Rush a lot more engaging than a regular idle game, all the player would have to do is build up enough money to get one bomb, and then you couldn't possibly lose your run. The "meta" had become almost the opposite of what I wanted Gold Rush to be in the first place.

With that in mind, I planned on making a game that was a lot more involved throughout an entire playthrough rather than up until a certain point of the game's progression. So I started messing around with making the player type words.

I wanted some sort of enemy for the player to fight, but I wasn't quite sure what I wanted the enemies to be yet. Once I had the typing system down, I wanted a way to upgrade your stuff, so I added a levelling system for both the player and the keyboard itself.

I then added "conditions" to enemies, making them have certain effects. For example, "Hard of Hearing" would mean you'd have to toggle Caps Lock to deal damage to an enemy. Or a resistance to vowels would mean your damage is cut in half every time you type one. This gave enemies some amount of variety, but I still needed to figure out what I wanted the enemies themselves to look like. And by this point, I still didn't have a visual style for the game.

After adding some items, I had an idea for the enemies and what I wanted them to represent: computer viruses. I figured since the player was typing to deal damage to something, maybe they're like their own antivirus, or something like that.

[DECLASSIFIED]

Keystroke's enemy scene in the Godot Engine. Each enemy has a type of virus that it represents, as well as a condition. They also type words to damage the player in return.


This is also how I found the game's visual style. I had an idea for an adware enemy where you have to close out of pop-ups that block your vision. When I started doing the artwork, it ended up looking almost like a Windows98-esq app window with an enemy inside. I ultimately decided to center the game's entire visual style around this concept, and I think it looks pretty solid.

[DECLASSIFIED]

A full screenshot of Keystroke with the stylized UI implemented. Designed to look like Windows98.


My next big challenge was adding a shop system that I was happy with. I knew I wanted the player to be able to buy utility items like health potions, but I also wanted to be able to buy words to add to your word bank. The words were not very hard to add. All that took was adding buttons, calculaing a cost depending on the word, and "applying" that word to a button to make the word purchasable. Once the basic structure was finished, I wanted to work on being able to make items efficiently. To do this, I made a resource that has an item name, price, and some other values. This resource is used as a template for every item in the game. All I had to do was make a new item, fill in all the info, and the shop system's code handles the rest.

[DECLASSIFIED]

A filled in item resource in Keystroke's Godot project. Each field is filled in with some info for the game to display in the shop.


Making it so the player can actually use the items was more difficult. I wasn't sure how to do it efficiently, so I just took what I did for Gold Rush and made a row that the player can scroll through and click to use the items. This was annoying to do as I haven't used many control nodes prior to this, but I did eventually figure it out.

[DECLASSIFIED]

The Godot scene tree of the game's item selector. The player can scroll down to find more items to use.


Early on in this project, I knew I wanted to have music in this game. But there was a slight problem, and it's that I know almost nobody who makes music. I think I can count the number of people on one hand. The best person I thought I could message for this was my friend Winston. He's made these sort of lofi hip-hop beats before, so I figured it couldn't hurt to ask him to work on the soundtrack. He agreed to do it, and put together the first song on a Discord call in half an hour. That song ultimately ended up being the shop's theme. Maybe a week or so later, the soundtrack was done, and I put out the game on itch.io.


I learned a lot from this project. It was my second game I've actually finished. And I put some amount of thought into how to make a lot of items easily. Yeah, it is very far from perfect, but I'm still proud of this. If you've played it, I truly do appreciate it. And if you've somehow read through all of this, thank you. It took a while to write this but it was fun, so I can't complain too much.
